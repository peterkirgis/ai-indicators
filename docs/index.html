How NYT Readers Feel About AI

Each comment counts equally

Most NYT readers are skeptical of AI — and have been since day one

Since ChatGPT launched in November 2022, negative sentiment has consistently dominated reader comments. Despite major capability advances — GPT-4, Sora, DeepSeek — the ratio has remained remarkably stable: roughly half of commenters express fear, concern, or criticism of AI.

Representative negative comments

Most people see AI as a tool humans control — not an autonomous agent

When commenters describe AI, the majority treat it as a technology to be used, shaped, and regulated rather than as an entity with its own agency or will. This framing has also held steady over time, suggesting a stable conceptual model of AI in the public mind.

Comments framing AI as a tool

But those who see AI as an agent are far more fearful

Commenters who frame AI as an autonomous entity — writing as if AI “wants,” “thinks,” or “is coming for us” — are dramatically more negative than those who treat AI as a tool. Entity framers show roughly twice the rate of negative sentiment, suggesting that anthropomorphization and fear go hand in hand.

Comments framing AI as an autonomous entity

The optimists: what does enthusiasm about AI look like?

The minority of commenters who are positive about AI tend to emphasize its potential as a productivity tool, draw parallels to past technological transitions, or express excitement about scientific and creative possibilities.

Methodology

Data collection

Articles: Extracted from the NYT Archive API (Nov 2022 – Mar 2026). Headlines filtered for mentions of “AI,” “A.I.,” or “artificial intelligence” using regex pattern matching. 1,434 articles matched.

Comments: All reader comments scraped via the NYT Community API, including replies. Comments sorted by “recommended” (default NYT ordering). 138,450 comments collected.

Sentiment & framing classification

Each comment was classified by Google Gemini 3 Flash (via OpenRouter, temperature 0.0) on two dimensions:

Sentiment — the commenter’s attitude toward AI itself (not the article):
• Positive: enthusiasm, optimism, support
• Negative: fear, concern, criticism, opposition
• Neutral: factual discussion, genuine questions
• Irrelevant: not about AI

Framing — how the commenter conceptualizes AI:
• Tool: AI as technology humans use and control
• Entity: AI as an autonomous agent with its own will or agency
• Neither: doesn’t clearly fit either category

Comments were truncated to 500 characters and processed in batches of 50. Each classification includes a confidence level (high/medium/low).

Community-endorsed (weighted) analysis

The weighted view adjusts for two biases in raw comment counts:

1. Not all comments are equal. Likes (recommendations) indicate community endorsement. We use log(1 + likes) to weight each comment, compressing the influence of viral outliers while still reflecting community preference.

2. Early comments get more likes. The first comments on an article accumulate disproportionately more likes simply due to visibility. We correct for this by dividing each comment’s weight by 1 + 2.0 × position_rank, where position_rank ranges from 0.0 (first comment) to 1.0 (last). This applies at most a 3× correction.

Replies (depth > 1) and comments with zero likes are excluded from the weighted view.

Error bands

The shaded bands on the sentiment chart show the 25th–75th percentile range of per-article sentiment percentages within each month. Only articles with ≥ 5 classified comments are included. Wider bands indicate more variation between articles in that month; narrow bands mean articles that month had similar sentiment distributions.

Geographic analysis & reference data

Commenter locations are self-reported free text, normalized to US states using pattern matching (state names, abbreviations, “City, ST” patterns, and a dictionary of ~100 major US cities). Coverage: ~65% of comments mapped to a US state. States with fewer than 50 classified comments are excluded.

Education: Percentage of adults 25+ with a bachelor’s degree or higher, from the US Census Bureau American Community Survey 2023 1-year estimates (Table S1501). DC is excluded from the scatter plot as an outlier (62.5%).
GDP per capita: Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), 2023 state-level GDP per capita in current dollars. DC is excluded from the GDP scatter plot as an outlier ($236K).

Limitations

• Single news source (NYT) with a specific readership demographic
• LLM-based classification may introduce systematic biases vs. human annotators
• Comment populations skew toward engaged, opinionated readers
• Like counts reflect NYT readership preferences, not the general public
• Early months (Nov–Dec 2022) have fewer articles, increasing variance
